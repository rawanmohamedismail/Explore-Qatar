Where is Qatar?

The Pearl of the Arabian Peninsula

Qatar — pronounced 'kuh-TAR' — is a small peninsula jutting northward from the northeastern corner of the Arabian Peninsula into the warm, clear waters of the Persian Gulf. It lies between latitudes 24° and 27°N and longitudes 50° and 52°E.

The country shares its only land border — just 87 km long — with Saudi Arabia to the south. A narrow strait of around 40 km separates Qatar from the island nation of Bahrain to the west. Qatar is small enough to explore entirely in a single trip, yet rich enough in landscapes and experiences to reward any amount of time spent here.

Despite its modest size of 11,586 km², Qatar is a country of extraordinary geographical variety — flat limestone plateaus in the north, sweeping desert plains in the south, 563 km of pristine coastline, and one of the most unusual natural wonders on earth: Khor Al Adaid, the Inland Sea, where towering sand dunes plunge directly into the sea.

Did You Know?

Qatar has no rivers and receives only about 10 cm of rain per year on average. The country relies on seawater desalination plants — among the world's largest — to supply its drinking water.

Landscapes

Qatar's Diverse Natural Terrain

From singing dunes and ancient limestone hills to mangrove forests and crystal Gulf waters — Qatar's natural landscapes are endlessly surprising.

The Desert

Qatar's interior is dominated by flat, low-lying desert — golden sand plains, crescent-shaped dunes that move up to 10 metres per year, and the hamada (rocky desert) formed by weathered bedrock. Qatar's famous "singing dunes" produce an eerie vibrating hum when the sand moves.

Khor Al Adaid — The Inland Sea

A UNESCO-recognised natural reserve where towering sand dunes cascade directly into the sea. One of the world's most spectacular and unique geographical phenomena.

563 km Coastline

White-sand beaches, secluded coves, coral reefs and 10 offshore islands dot Qatar's Arabian Gulf coast. Clear turquoise waters teem with marine life year-round.

Mangroves & Wetlands

Al Thakhira, Qatar's oldest and largest mangrove reserve, is a serene ecosystem of waterways and bird life. A kayaker's paradise in the cooler months.

Limestone Plateaus & Jebels

Qatar's highest point — Qurain Abu al-Bawl at 103 m — sits among limestone jebels (hills) famous for 900+ ancient petroglyphs carved by Neolithic inhabitants.

Sabkha — Salt Flats

Shimmering white salt flats form where seawater meets low-lying land. On the road to the Inland Sea, sabkhas create an otherworldly, mirror-like landscape at dawn.

Natural Resources

Beneath the Surface

Qatar sits atop extraordinary natural wealth. The country holds the world's third-largest natural gas reserves — between 10% and 14% of all known global reserves — shared with Iran through the offshore North Field, the single largest natural gas reservoir on earth.

In 2023, Qatar's proved crude oil reserves were estimated at 25.2 billion barrels, making it the 6th largest reserve in the Middle East and 14th largest globally. Oil was first struck at the Dukhan Field in 1939, transforming Qatar from a pearl-diving economy into one of the world's wealthiest nations within a single generation.

Qatar is also the world's largest exporter of liquefied natural gas (LNG). Its main energy trading partners include Japan, South Korea, India, China, and the United States. This resource wealth has funded the country's extraordinary investment in infrastructure, culture, sport, and education.

Desalinated Water

Nature

Qatar's Natural Wonders

Qatar's unique peninsula is one of stark contrasts and fascinating natural wonders — from prehistoric petroglyphs to coral reefs teeming with life.

Khor Al Adaid — The Inland Sea

Where the desert meets the sea in a breathtaking spectacle — towering golden dunes tumble directly into the crystal waters of the Arabian Gulf. A UNESCO-recognised reserve and the crown jewel of Qatar's natural landscape. Accessible by 4x4 only, it's an unmissable experience.

Al Thakhira Mangroves

Qatar's oldest and largest mangrove reserve, near Al Khor in the north. A tranquil paradise of winding waterways, lush green vegetation, and rich birdlife. Ideal for kayaking, birdwatching, and peaceful nature walks. Best visited between October and April.

Coral Reefs & Marine Life

Qatar is the richest Gulf country in coral reefs. The best reefs are found along the northeastern coast and around Al Aaliya Island. Home to hawksbill turtles, dugongs, whale sharks, dolphins, and over 300 species of fish. Qatar's Gulf waters are truly extraordinary.

Al Jassasiya Petroglyphs

Fossilised limestone jebels are home to nearly 900 ancient petroglyphs — rock carvings dating to Neolithic times. Featuring etchings of dhow boats, pearl holders, ostriches, and ancient board games, Al Jassasiya is one of the most significant archaeological sites in the Gulf.

Wadis & Desert Oases

Qatar's dry river valleys — wadis — come alive during brief winter rains, attracting diverse communities of birds, geckos, hedgehogs, snakes, and desert rodents. Green oases at Purple Island and Al Thakhira offer surprising pockets of lush vegetation in the arid landscape.

Dahl Al Misfir Cave

A spectacular underground cave formed as groundwater dissolved carbonate rock over millennia. As this underground pocket fills during winter rains, life follows — foxes, bats, and birds find refuge in this extraordinary geological formation in Qatar's interior.
